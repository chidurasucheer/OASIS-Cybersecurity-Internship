 # Research Report on Social Engineering Attacks

## 📌 Objective
To research and document the various methods used in social engineering attacks, analyze real-world case studies, and provide recommendations for prevention.

---

## 🔍 A. Types of Social Engineering

* **Phishing:** The most common form of social engineering, where attackers send fraudulent communications (usually emails) that appear to come from a reputable source to steal sensitive data like login credentials or credit card numbers.
* **Pretexting:** An attacker creates a fabricated scenario (a pretext) to trick a victim into revealing personal or corporate information. They often pretend to be from a government agency, bank, or IT department to establish trust.
* **Baiting:** Similar to phishing, but it uses a physical "bait" to pique a victim's curiosity. For example, an attacker might leave a malware-infected USB drive in a public place like a parking lot or lobby, waiting for someone to plug it into their system.

---

## 🏛️ B. Case Studies & Impact

### **Case Study: The 2020 Twitter Bitcoin Hack**
In July 2020, attackers used phone-based social engineering to target several Twitter employees. By pretending to be from the internal IT department, they gained access to administrative tools, which allowed them to hijack high-profile accounts (like those of Barack Obama and Elon Musk) to promote a Bitcoin scam.

### **Impact**
* **Financial Loss:** Organizations can lose millions due to direct theft or legal fines.
* **Data Breaches:** Loss of sensitive customer and employee data.
* **Brand Reputation:** A total loss of customer trust that can take years to recover.

---

## 🛡️ C. Prevention Recommendations

1.  **Security Awareness Training:** Regularly educating employees on how to spot suspicious emails, unusual phone calls, or "too good to be true" offers.
2.  **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):** Implementing MFA ensures that even if a password is stolen via phishing, the attacker cannot access the account without the second factor.
3.  **Verification Protocols:** Establishing strict policies that require employees to verify the identity of anyone requesting sensitive data through a secondary, trusted channel (e.g., calling back a known official number).
